Cybersecurity Analyst
https://youtu.be/EwpcwMz1c0Q

Cybersecurity analysts study and respond to anomalies discovered and generated by computer usage and internet browsing around the world. One of the challenges they face is the vast volume of logs and events that arrive each day to be analysed. This might be anything from 5000 to 15,000 log entries per second, or over 150,000 for a much larger organisation, and this is work that takes up most of the analyst's time. Most occurrences are sorted by software, which either flags them as safe or stamps them as alerts if they pose a concern. The alerts are then forwarded to the cybersecurity analyst, who will review and triage them to decide whether someone is a threat or has committed a crime. Cybersecurity analysts communicate with top-level colleagues, fellow analysts, and system users daily to complete their tasks. They spend their days at workstations working on computers in a security operation centre (SOC) surrounded by monitors that alert them to potential threats.

Data Scientist
https://youtu.be/xC-c7E5PK0Y

A data scientist is someone who collects, analyses, and cleans data. They use machine learning to make decisions and compare data from multiple sources to identify patterns and trends. They plan projects, solve problems, strategize, research, recommend, generate graphics, and model implementations to identify the optimal course for a company's growth and success. Depending on the size of the company they have been hired to work for, they could be the project's single data scientist responsible for all aspects of the project, or they could be working with a team of software engineers, data engineers, research scientists, and fellow data scientists, delegating tasks and focusing their time on what they do best, as well as working closely with the client. Outlining realistic challenges and devising acceptable methodologies for evaluating solutions is one of the most difficult components of being a data scientist. Data scientists can work from home or in the office because most of their work involves analysis.

Programmer
https://youtu.be/E7TFbPRyi-c

Computer programming is a position that necessitates a continually evolving skill set to keep up with the ever-changing world of technology. A client will employ a computer programmer to write code in several languages, such as Python, C++, and Java, and expects their computer software to function in a smooth and reliable manner. By assessing the clientâ€™s wants and needs a programmer will convert program designs created by a production team of designers and engineers into computer-readable instructions, the programmer will then create, develop, and test the product whether it be a website, game, or phone app. This can now be done anywhere, including from home, on-site, or within an IT department. They will keep updating their projects to stay current with technology, and they will endeavour to address and debug any issues that surface once the software is published and in use.

A programmer may also be employed to test existing websites and applications to ensure that they are working smoothly and efficiently. Meeting the demands of a clientele is one of the most difficult tasks a programmer will encounter. If a customer is unsure of what they want as a finished product or is constantly changing specifications in the middle of a project, the programmer will have to overcome these obstacles to accomplish the assignment within the deadlines allocated to them.

Service Desk Analyst
https://youtu.be/dEOCo7mYYuA

Communicating with clients experiencing technical issues with their telecommunication devices and networks is the primary task of an IT Service Desk Analyst. They are within the support team consulted to assess a problem, and either provide a solution or escalate to a technical specialist. Service desk analysts use a variety of technologies and programmes to deliver timely and thorough technical and continuing assistance. Analysts spend the majority of their time in a call centre or at an office service desk, yet they are also expected to attend customer and management meetings and collaborate with their co-workers. They are continually educated to keep their knowledge up to date within the ever-changing world of technology and customer service. The enormous volume of calls and emails that analysts must deal with daily is one of the main challenges they may face, along with challenging customers.

A Technical writer is hired to document how a product works in a straightforward, precise, and clear manner to their client's target audience. The most challenging and main responsibility is to conduct extensive research to become as knowledgeable as possible on a specific product, its target audience, and its requirements, ensuring that the information is accurate, up to date, and relevant. Technical writers participate to brainstorm, write drafts, revise and edit project documentation, while taking care to only include information that is essential to the reader. They ensure their writing is consistent throughout each document, using familiar terms and styles to avoid confusing the reader. Jargon is only used, when necessary, where it is applicable to the target audience. Being writers, they also check their grammar, punctuation, and spelling frequently and thoroughly.

Tabs, bullet points, clear and easy-to-understand visual aids that make sense to the audience, proper spacing, and a nice blank background should all be included to make the content easy to read. Technical writers aren't just restricted to web pages or internal documentation, they also write user documentation and manuals, sometimes by generating content that will be saved as a PDF or a help file. Technical writers may also do all the writing for whitepapers, and technical statements for press releases.
