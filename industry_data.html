Labour insight data was made available to the team in the form of a selection of charts from Burning Glass technologies. This information is used here to discuss the relevance of this data to the Simply Code team considering team member preferences for an ideal job. The data itself may be considered a little out of date given none of the data provided is newer than March 2018, however, it still serves as a useful tool with which to contrast team member expectations regarding their career preferences, and to determine if – given the data presented – any team member would be likely to change their minds as to whether they should pursue a different career specialty.

Job Titles & Occupations

From the ideal jobs that team members described in their Assignment 1 reports, several skills, job titles, and occupations were identified for comparison with the Burning Glass data. Three key occupations were identified in the team member’s ideal jobs advertisements: Software Developer, Cybersecurity Consultant, and Naval Communications and Information Systems (CIS) Operator. Per the Burning Glass (2018a) Top IT Jobs data, only the Software Developers had selected jobs with titles considered to be demand, or with the required skills needed to satisfy such job titles. Of the 25 job titles listed, 11 can be considered developer roles (as opposed to engineering) and reduces to 8 when identifying entry level roles suited to the team members at the completion of their higher education. Also of interest is the spread of job titles, which causes the data to appear somewhat misleading. While the most demanded job title was listed as Solutions Architect, the combined developer titles show that while certain job titles may be less in demand, the requirement for software developer roles is significantly greater than for most other job titles in the IT sector.

This rudimentary analysis compares well to the Top Occupations data (Burning Glass 2018b), which, while it also separates several software development roles, shows a clear bias towards such occupations in terms of employer demand, specifically with the software developer/engineer occupation ranked highest in the data. The Cybersecurity Consultant choice also finds itself as an in-demand occupation at the 17th position in the rankings, and while there are comparably less matches in terms of the data, this may signify the role as more specialised that some of the other occupations listed. According to the Royal Australian Navy Warfare Sailors Career Handbook, a part of the CIS Officer’s role is the management of shipboard and onshore computer networks as well as other communication systems (RAN 2007), which implies a skillset that is – in part – comparable to that of a Network Engineer (ranked 19th in the data) and may therefore also be considered an in-demand occupation according to Burning Glass.

General Skills

The Hays Salary Guide for 2020/21 indicates (Hays 2021) that most ANZ employers across all sectors of the labour market currently view soft skills as equally important to the technical skills, with the top three skills being Teamwork, Problem Solving, and Communication. This appears to be a trend that has continued since the Burning Glass (2018c) data was extracted, where such “baseline” skills were listed within the top 5 ranked skills, communication being ranked 1st and problem solving a somewhat distant 2nd. While the team’s ideal jobs listed several different general skills, between them they were likewise advertised with a combined emphasis on the same soft skills as the team’s top 3 most required general skills.

The Burning Glass (2018c) data appears to show a significant difference between the top 2 most demanded soft skills at a ratio close to 3:1, however even though the team sample size is so small, the same difference between skills required for the team’s ideal jobs was not present, with the top 3 identified soft skills in parity. Leadership, Mentoring, and Team Building were the top 3 identified skills in the Burning Glass data which were not required for the team’s ideal jobs, and these soft skills ranked 11th, 13th, and 18th respectively. This means that the team has sought roles with the top 10 general skills that were most in demand in IT in 2018.

IT Industry Skills

Narrowing down the team’s top 3 in demand IT specialised skills is a little less obvious, since a large proportion of common skills in the team’s jobs were split evenly between different team members, and because many of the team’s most common ideal job skills are not explicitly named in the Burning Glass (2018d) data. There are parallels though, and therefore the top identified IT skills that match the burning Glass data are: JavaScript, SQL, and Software Engineering, ranked at positions 2, 1, and 15 respectively. This result is entirely focused on the larger proportion of team members pursuing a software development pathway, in part because the software developer roles were advertised with an exhaustive list of technology skills required for those jobs, whereas the cybersecurity and CIS job advertisements required very few IT specific skills, and those listed were primarily network and infrastructure focused. This disparity reinforces the view that the non-programmer jobs are likely to be more specialised in terms of overall skillset.

When reviewing the Burning Glass data for skills in demand that the team does not require, the options were much easier to identify. Knowledge of Microsoft Windows, Project Management, and SAP were ranked 4th, 5th, and 6th by Burning Glass (2018d), yet this still meant that some team members individually required skills in the top 3 in demand skills in the data. It could be expected that the data would once again skew heavily in the direction of the software developers given the large differences in demand for programmers vs non-programmers, however, the IT skills results were surprising given the promising outcomes noted in favour of the team in respect to the occupations and general skills data noted above. Perhaps employers may have searched for a very specific set of skills for their IT employees, however this would not explain why several skills – that were not limited specifically to IT roles – have appeared in the Burning Glass IT skills data.

Skills such as Building Relationships, Customer Service, and Business Process may require staff to have some IT related skills, yet they do not necessarily need to be IT focused. The Burning Glass (2018d) data also lists Microsoft Windows and Microsoft Office, skills usually associated with roles that are not typically IT occupations and would generally favour the business software that companies prefer for business administration and other non-IT specific work. This highlights a problem with the sampling method for the data, as there are many IT skills required by non-IT occupations, and these are likely to pollute any data intended to target IT industry occupations. On the surface this may seem that the data has no value to the team, however, this is far from the case. It is notable that non-IT specific occupations rely so heavily on the software and systems that are created and managed by those who choose an IT career. The Australian Government (2018) notes that technology skills are an educational priority to ensure that business can employ a workforce with IT skills that are transferrable, allowing businesses to transform how they operate as the digital economy continues to both support and drive such change. This implies a significant market demand for information technologies, which further implies a growing demand for the specialised skills required to create those same technologies.

Team Reflections

On reviewing the Burning Glass data, the team reflected individually about whether the industry data might change their opinions regarding their ideal jobs, and each contributed the paragraphs that follow. The overwhelming consensus, however, is that the team’s skills are transferrable, and each team member is keen to pursue a career that they are passionate about.

Sean comments that the Burning Glass Data is considerably out of date, and the sampling method is questionable given the granular separation of job titles and occupations which does not appear to reflect a deeper understanding of the bigger picture requirements of IT industry. As an example, one might be drawn to conclude that there was a greater demand for solutions architects in 2018, however, the number of developer roles combined – not including software engineers – clearly outnumbered the solutions architects by a ratio of 9.5:1. Job demand is certainly important, particularly when first commencing a chosen career path, however a love for the job, and a genuine interested in the work, the company, and its culture is perhaps even more important. While the Burning Glass data itself is already inadequate to sway the decision to choose a developer role as an ideal job, research into the chosen company and with regards to the work required will if anything further cement my decision as being both appropriate, and well considered.

For Marius, the Burning Glass data has reinforced his career choice as a Software Developer, rated as the number one in-demand occupation. Although it only comes up as the 19th job title, he found other job titles where the same IT skillset is required. The data has also helped him understand the most in-demand IT skills, which will help inform his decisions as he progresses through his Bachelor of IT.

Kiran states that a review of the industry data did not result in a change of opinion. Choosing the best job is more than merely looking at what is in demand. There is a consensus in society that money cannot buy happiness. While it is important to choose a long-term viable career, many of the skills required for the chosen job are transferable, both the general and the IT skills. In the event of needing to reskill, this job would place any IT professional in a good position to transition. From writing reports, to understanding IT systems, the Block Chain developer is solid waypoint in an IT career.

Charles reflects that after looking at the data from Burning Glass his view regarding his ideal job has changed slightly. He is yet to make up his mind on what path he would like to follow, however from the data provided he has seen that even though the title may have changed the skills require have not. The goals Charles has set remain relevant; to learn and grow as much as possible throughout the journey that he has only recently set out on.

Tiarna realised that the Burning Glass data was not entirely relevant to her desired navy position after reading it. Although there are many similarities with security and communications, she is not just focused on an IT career, but rather one in the military, where the goals and job opportunities are significantly different. Even though it is not one of the higher-paying professions, serving in the Australian defence force has many benefits for servicemen/women and their families. 

Trill determined that by examining the data available to her from Burning Glass, it is apparent that Security Analyst, or Security Consultant was listed low in the Occupations and not at all in demand amongst the Job Titles. This is an interesting discovery for Trill who was expecting demand for cybersecurity skills to be much higher. This has not changed Trill’s opinion regarding her job preference, because the skills used in that occupation are applicable for other, highly in-demand jobs.
